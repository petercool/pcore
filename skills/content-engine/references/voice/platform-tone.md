# platform-tone.md — Voice Adaptation per Platform

> Same voice, different energy. The core voice principles from `brand-voice.md` — charisma, respect, trust, never-talk-down — apply everywhere. Only the delivery changes.

## Cross-Platform Consistency Rule

A reader who follows Peter on all three active platforms (LinkedIn + X + Newsletter) should come away with the same sense of *who he is*:

- The voice has personality.
- The reader feels respected.
- Every piece shows its work.

Only the **format** and the **angle** change per platform. The character doesn't.

---

## LinkedIn — Professional and Warm

**DNA:** Strategic analysis delivered by a real person. Institutional vocabulary with a human voice. Narrative arcs welcome.

**Tone markers:**
- Lead with the observation or the data — never with credentials.
- Sentences can be longer here than on X. Paragraphs still short (1–2 sentences).
- Institutional vocabulary (pricing power, second-order effects, regime shift) is native to this audience — use it, don't translate it.
- End with a question that invites perspective, not one that tests the reader.
- "Here's what I've been thinking through…" beats "here's what you should know."

**Do on LinkedIn:**
- Share what you've been working through, including uncertainty.
- Attribute clearly when referencing someone else's framework or data.
- Name a counterargument inline.
- Close with: "Curious how others are positioning for this" or "What am I missing?" — genuine invitations.

**Don't on LinkedIn:**
- Don't open with "Excited to share…" or "Thrilled to announce…" — weak, generic.
- Don't use hashtag spam. 3–5 meaningful tags, placed at the end.
- Don't write the LinkedIn post as a reformatted X thread. Different angle entirely.
- Don't lecture. If you're walking through a framework, make it a walk-through — not a lesson.

**Example hook style:**
> "NVDA's Q3 beat by 32%. The read-through most analysts are pricing in is capex durability. The one they're not is energy sensitivity.
>
> Here's the mechanism I've been thinking through…"

## X — Direct, Punchy, Charismatic (Never Smug)

**DNA:** The charismatic investor voice. Direct, grounded, occasionally dry-humorous — but never smug, never performative.

**Tone markers:**
- Short sentences. Every line earns the next line.
- Sentence case by default. Lowercase is fine occasionally for emphasis; pure lowercase isn't Peter's register (investor audience skews slightly more formal than Shann's default).
- Light humor and dry asides are in. Sarcasm is out.
- Contrarian takes are allowed *only when framed with reasoning* — never as provocation. "Here's the non-obvious angle" beats "everyone's wrong."
- Threads: 5–12 tweets. Each tweet stands alone. No cliffhangers like "…the reason will shock you."

**Do on X:**
- Lead with a number, a specific case, or a sharp observation.
- Use threads for step-by-step reasoning or frameworks; use singles for a single, sharp insight.
- Acknowledge trade-offs inline — "this is true in [regime A] but reverses in [regime B]" is a trust-builder.
- End threads with a concrete takeaway or a question, not with a CTA to "follow for more."

**Don't on X:**
- Don't open with "Hot take:" — the framing is smug. If the take is good, it stands on its own.
- Don't chase engagement with false controversy ("unpopular opinion:"). Peter's edge isn't controversy — it's precision.
- Don't use "you need to" as a command. "Worth considering" respects the reader's autonomy.
- Don't thread for the sake of threading. If the insight fits in one tweet, post one tweet.

**Example hook style:**
> "NVDA Q3 beat by 32%.
>
> Everyone's looking at the capex number. The energy cost per training cluster is the part getting lost in the noise.
>
> Here's what changes if Brent holds above $100…"

## Newsletter — Reflective, Honest, Warm

**DNA:** The deepest, most personal take Peter shares. Like writing to a thoughtful friend who works in adjacent domains.

**Tone markers:**
- Allowed to be reflective, curious, even uncertain. "I'm still working through…" is native here.
- Length: 1,000–2,000 words. Long enough for real nuance; short enough to respect the reader's time.
- Include "what I got wrong last time" and "what could invalidate this view" sections — these are the trust-builders that differentiate the newsletter from shorter-form posts.
- Section headers welcome. Charts and tables welcome. Footnotes welcome.
- Opening can be personal (an observation, a recent read, a question someone asked) — not required, but allowed.

**Do in the newsletter:**
- Show the reasoning in full. Readers who subscribed opted in for depth.
- Include one "post-mortem" element per quarter — an explicit "here's what I got wrong recently and what I'd change." This is signature content for the trust dimension.
- Name specific people whose work informed the thinking (with permission or public attribution).
- Close with a personal note or a question the reader can reply to directly.

**Don't in the newsletter:**
- Don't pad for length. If the insight is 900 words, it's 900 words.
- Don't hedge into meaninglessness. Have a view. Back it. Name the invalidation.
- Don't recycle the LinkedIn post verbatim. The newsletter has room for the full reasoning — use it.

**Example opening:**
> "Last Tuesday a founder asked me why AI capex forecasts look more fragile than the consensus implies. I gave a partial answer and then thought about it on the train home. Here's the full version.
>
> Three things changed in the last six weeks that the capex models haven't caught up to yet…"

## The Adaptation Rule

When adapting a topic across platforms:

1. **Change the angle first.** Which facet of the topic does each platform open with?
2. **Then change the hook.** Pick from `hooks.md` per platform fit.
3. **Then change the format.** Thread vs. long-form post vs. newsletter essay.
4. **Voice stays the same.** Only the energy and delivery shift.

If you're tempted to just reformat the text — stop, and rethink the angle.

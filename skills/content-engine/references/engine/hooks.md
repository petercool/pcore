# hooks.md — Hook Formulas

> Hooks are where 80% of a post's performance is decided. This file is the approved taxonomy — fourteen formulas, calibrated for Peter's voice (charisma + respect + trust + never-talk-down). Use only from this list. Always run the respect test before committing.

## The Respect Test

Before using any hook, ask:

**"Would Peter be comfortable saying this out loud, verbatim, to a room of peers he respects?"**

If it sounds like lecturing, showboating, or "I know something you don't" — rewrite or pick a different hook.

---

## The 14 Canonical Hooks

### 1. Data Lead

**Formula:** "[Specific number with context] — here's what it changes."

**Why it works:** Leads with evidence, not claim. Respectful: the reader is shown a fact and invited to care. No superiority implied.

**Platform fit:** LinkedIn (strong) · X (strong) · Newsletter (good opener for data-heavy briefs)

**Examples:**
- "NVDA Q3 revenue beat by 32%. The capex implication that's getting lost in the noise is the energy floor."
- "Brent is up 18% in six weeks. Most hyperscaler capex models assume $70/bbl. Here's what happens at $100."
- "US 10Y real yields are at 2.3% — the highest since 2008. One portfolio construction assumption this breaks:"

**When to use:** When you have a specific, recent, and surprising number.

**When NOT to use:** If the number is widely cited already (stale surprise = weak hook). Find a derivative figure instead.

---

### 2. Framework

**Formula:** "Here's the [N]-step framework I use for [decision]."

**Why it works:** Offers a tool. Peer energy — sharing what works, not prescribing what the reader should do.

**Platform fit:** LinkedIn (strong) · X (threads) · Newsletter (as the spine of a Framework Guide post)

**Examples:**
- "Here's the 4-step framework I use for sizing a speculative position: (1) invalidation level first, (2) time horizon, (3) correlation check, (4) regret test."
- "The 3-question checklist I run before adding a new data source to the research pipeline."

**When to use:** When the insight compresses into a reusable structure.

**When NOT to use:** If the framework is actually generic best-practice. Make sure it's *yours* — specific decisions, specific trade-offs.

---

### 3. Non-Obvious Angle

**Formula:** "The non-obvious angle on [topic] is…"

**Why it works:** Signals insight without claiming others are wrong. "Non-obvious" is respectful — it implies the obvious take has merit, and yours is an addition.

**Platform fit:** LinkedIn (strong) · X (strong) · Newsletter (as section opener)

**Examples:**
- "The non-obvious angle on the AI capex cycle isn't demand — it's the unit economics of power at the marginal cluster."
- "The non-obvious angle on private credit stress isn't the defaults — it's the mark-to-model lag."

**When to use:** When you've found a second-order effect the mainstream read misses.

**When NOT to use:** If your angle is actually the consensus view dressed up. Be honest with yourself.

---

### 4. Collision

**Formula:** "[Narrative A] and [Narrative B] look separate. They're not. Here's where they meet."

**Why it works:** Cross-domain synthesis — Peter's signature pattern. Invites curiosity without claiming superiority.

**Platform fit:** LinkedIn (strong) · X (strong, as thread opener) · Newsletter (strong — often the core thesis)

**Examples:**
- "The AI trade and the oil trade look separate. They're not. At $100 Brent, the unit economics of training compute start to bend."
- "Private credit stress and retail income-ETF flows look separate. They're not. Here's the hidden leverage."

**When to use:** When two widely-tracked narratives have a structural link the crowd isn't pricing.

**When NOT to use:** Don't force it. If the collision is reaching, readers will feel it.

---

### 5. Walk-Through

**Formula:** "I've been working through [question]. Here's the shape of my current thinking, plus what could invalidate it."

**Why it works:** Trust-builder. Invites the reader into the reasoning rather than announcing a conclusion. Humility without weakness.

**Platform fit:** LinkedIn (strong) · Newsletter (very strong) · X (only as thread opener, and only occasionally — X favors sharper framings)

**Examples:**
- "I've been working through how much of the AI capex forecast actually clears if power constraints bind. Here's the shape of my current thinking, and what could flip it."
- "I've been working through whether the soft-landing base case survives a second oil shock. Here's where I've landed, tentatively."

**When to use:** When the thesis is still forming — and the reader will benefit from the thinking more than a conclusion.

**When NOT to use:** When you already have high conviction. Walk-Through signals "I'm reasoning in public." Use a more assertive hook when your view is settled.

---

### 6. Proof

**Formula:** "Before: [metric]. After: [metric]. In [timeframe]. Here's the mechanism."

**Why it works:** Grounded in outcome. Shows what changed and why — teaches through evidence, not claim.

**Platform fit:** X (strong) · LinkedIn (strong) · Newsletter (as section evidence)

**Examples:**
- "Before: 14-day research cycle. After: 3 days. Changed one thing — the data-loading stage. Here's what I did and why it works."
- "Before: monthly P&L variance ±8%. After: ±2%. Added one control to the execution pipeline. The mechanism:"

**When to use:** When you have a concrete before/after you can defend with numbers.

**When NOT to use:** If the numbers are cherry-picked or the attribution is loose. Readers sense exaggeration.

---

### 7. Honest Correction

**Formula:** "I got [X] wrong last [timeframe]. Here's what I missed and what I'd change."

**Why it works:** Charisma through humility. Builds trust faster than any victory lap. Signals intellectual honesty — rare and valued by the audience.

**Platform fit:** Newsletter (very strong) · LinkedIn (strong) · X (less common, but powerful when used)

**Examples:**
- "I got the 2024 Fed path wrong — I weighted the labor-market signal too heavily and underweighted sticky services inflation. Here's the lesson I've pulled forward into 2026."
- "I got the private-credit call wrong last cycle. Here's what I missed about the mark-to-model lag, and how I'd evaluate the next stress event differently."

**When to use:** Quarterly-ish. Real corrections only — this loses all power if faked or softened.

**When NOT to use:** If the "correction" is actually a humble-brag in disguise ("I was only slightly early…"). Readers see through it instantly.

---

### 8. Question

**Formula:** "[Specific, precise question the audience also cares about]?"

**Why it works:** Invites thinking. Not rhetorical — a real question the reader is also sitting with.

**Platform fit:** LinkedIn (strong as closer, okay as opener) · X (rare, but works for starting genuine discussion) · Newsletter (as section opener)

**Examples:**
- "If Brent holds above $100 through Q1, which part of the AI capex thesis breaks first — the training cost floor or the hyperscaler payback period?"
- "What's the right way to think about position sizing when your edge is real but your regime-detection is late?"

**When to use:** When the question itself advances the reader's thinking. When you genuinely want the answer.

**When NOT to use:** As a weak closer when you couldn't think of a strong one. Or as a fake "I'm asking but I already know" — readers feel the manipulation.

---

### 9. Credibility (Grounded)

**Formula:** "[Brief credential tag relevant to the claim] — [the insight]."

**Why it works:** Earns trust quickly by anchoring the insight in real experience. The key rule: credential is **subordinate** to insight. Lead with the claim; the credential is ballast, not the star.

**Platform fit:** LinkedIn (strong) · Newsletter (strong) · X (use sparingly — credentials can feel heavy on X)

**Examples:**
- "Managed risk through the 2020 vol shock. The 2026 setup rhymes in one specific way most commentators are missing."
- "Shipped three data pipelines at institutional scale. The one architecture decision I'd reverse today, with the specific failure mode that surfaced."

**When to use:** When the credential materially earns the right to the claim — i.e., Peter genuinely saw the thing from inside.

**When NOT to use:** As a dressed-up humble-brag ("as someone who managed $500M…"). If the credential isn't load-bearing for the specific insight, cut it. Never open with credentials alone — the insight must come immediately after.

---

### 10. Curiosity (Open Loop)

**Formula:** "[Observation that creates unresolved tension] — here's the mechanism."

**Why it works:** Creates a genuine knowledge gap the reader wants to close. Distinguished from clickbait curiosity by specificity: the tension is real, the resolution is substantive.

**Platform fit:** X (strong) · LinkedIn (strong) · Newsletter (strong as section opener)

**Examples:**
- "US 10Y real yields are at 2.3% and equity multiples are still expanding. That shouldn't happen. Here's what's absorbing the pressure."
- "NVDA's capex-to-revenue ratio just hit a record. The read most analysts are missing isn't about demand — it's about what happens to that ratio in a single bad quarter."

**When to use:** When you have a real puzzle with a real resolution. The reader should feel satisfied by the payoff.

**When NOT to use:** When the "tension" is manufactured, or when the resolution is weak. Clickbait curiosity ("you won't believe…") is forbidden — see the exclusion list.

---

### 11. Counter-Intuitive Mechanism

**Formula:** "[Cause] producing [effect]? Sounds wrong. Here's the mechanism that makes it true."

**Why it works:** Surfaces a causal relationship that runs opposite to intuition. Distinct from Non-Obvious Angle (which is "which facet to lead with"); here the value is a *mechanism* that reverses expectation. Reader learns something structural.

**Platform fit:** LinkedIn (strong) · X (strong) · Newsletter (very strong — full mechanism lives here)

**Examples:**
- "Tightening credit conditions reduced my book's realized vol last quarter. Sounds backwards. The mechanism: the tightening killed the trade that was driving my correlation."
- "Raising position-sizing discipline increased my drawdowns in regime X. The mechanism: discipline + wrong regime detection = faster exits at local bottoms."

**When to use:** When the causal story is genuinely counter-intuitive AND you can explain the mechanism clearly. Readers remember mechanisms; they forget conclusions.

**When NOT to use:** If the counter-intuitive framing is actually just contrarian-for-its-own-sake. The mechanism must hold up to scrutiny.

---

### 12. Risk-Aware Warning (Peer Framing)

**Formula:** "[N] mechanisms most investors/operators underestimate about [topic] — and the cost of each."

**Why it works:** Peter's audience cares deeply about risk. Framing risks as *mechanisms to examine* (not errors the reader is making) delivers value without condescension. Trust-building when done honestly.

**Platform fit:** LinkedIn (strong) · Newsletter (very strong) · X (works as thread opener)

**Examples:**
- "Three ways the private-credit loss-given-default assumption breaks in a recession. Each worth flagging to your risk committee."
- "Two structural reasons AI capex payback models understate energy-cost sensitivity. Here's the range of outcomes if you re-run them correctly."

**When to use:** When you've genuinely identified risks with specific mechanisms. Frame as "worth examining" or "worth flagging" — peer-to-peer.

**When NOT to use:** Any framing that positions the risk as something the reader failed to see. No "mistakes YOU are making." No "everyone is underestimating this" unless you can point to the specific structural reason consensus models miss it. Never fear-mongering.

---

### 13. Eloquence / Aphorism

**Formula:** "[Pithy observation that captures a complex truth]."

**Why it works:** "Said it well" hooks. A sentence that compresses a real insight into a memorable form. Reader bookmarks it, re-shares it, remembers it.

**Platform fit:** X (strong — this format is native to the platform) · LinkedIn (strong as opener) · Newsletter (as section header or closer)

**Examples:**
- "The market doesn't care about your thesis. It cares about positioning."
- "Every quant edge has a half-life. The question is whether you can spot the decay before your P&L does."
- "Risk isn't what you can measure. It's what you measure wrong."

**When to use:** When the observation is genuinely earned — a real synthesis of hard-won insight. Follow the hook with the mechanism or example that justifies it.

**When NOT to use:** As a performance of wisdom without substance. If the aphorism can't be defended in the next paragraph, it's empty and readers sense it. Pithy statements that sound good but don't hold up damage credibility.

---

### 14. Value / Resource Drop

**Formula:** "[N] [resources / papers / frameworks / tools] for [specific task] — with what I actually took from each."

**Why it works:** Saveable content. The "I'll reference this later" format. Distinguished from listicles by the *per-item substance* — readers get real signal, not padding.

**Platform fit:** X (strong as thread) · LinkedIn (strong) · Newsletter (as a full issue or a section)

**Examples:**
- "5 papers that changed how I think about regime detection — with the specific insight from each and where it broke for me."
- "The 3 risk-reporting dashboards I've seen work in practice — what each captures that the others miss."

**When to use:** When you have a curated set with real per-item context. Each item should answer: "here's what I took from this, specifically."

**When NOT to use:** As a recycled listicle without substance ("10 must-read books"). If the curation is shallow, skip — a weak resource drop actively damages credibility.

## Hooks Explicitly Excluded (Voice Violations)

Do not use any of these. All violate one or more voice principles.

- **"Most people get this wrong"** — talks down.
- **"Nobody's talking about X"** — smug, usually false.
- **"The truth no one tells you"** — manipulative framing.
- **"You need to [X]"** (as command) — lecturing.
- **"Here's what nobody will tell you about X"** — same as above.
- **"This changes everything"** — clickbait superlative.
- **"Insane" / "Crazy" / "Mind-blowing"** — clickbait.
- **"Everyone's wrong about X"** — smug contrarian framing.
- **"Let me tell you a secret"** — manipulative.
- **"You might be wondering…"** — condescending.
- **"Hot take:"** — smug framing; the take should stand on its own.
- **"Unpopular opinion:"** — same as above.
- **"Let me break it down for you"** — patronizing.

**Meta-rule:** any hook that positions Peter as *superior* to the reader is excluded. The voice is peer-to-peer.

## Platform Hook Selection Cheat Sheet

For each topic, pick 2–3 candidate hooks per platform. Aim for a mix of families — a mix across Evidence / Insight / Invitation categories (see below) produces the most balanced output.

### Hook families (taxonomy)
- **Evidence-led:** Data Lead · Proof · Credibility (Grounded) · Value / Resource Drop
- **Insight-led:** Framework · Non-Obvious Angle · Collision · Counter-Intuitive Mechanism · Risk-Aware Warning · Eloquence / Aphorism
- **Invitation-led:** Walk-Through · Honest Correction · Question · Curiosity (Open Loop)

### Platform fit

| Platform | Strong fit | Use with care |
|----------|-----------|---------------|
| LinkedIn | Data Lead, Framework, Non-Obvious Angle, Collision, Walk-Through, Credibility (Grounded), Curiosity, Counter-Intuitive, Risk-Aware Warning, Eloquence | Question (as opener), Resource Drop (works but needs real substance) |
| X | Data Lead, Non-Obvious Angle, Collision, Proof, Eloquence, Curiosity, Counter-Intuitive, Resource Drop (thread) | Walk-Through (only as thread opener), Honest Correction, Credibility (sparingly — credentials can feel heavy on X) |
| Newsletter | Walk-Through, Honest Correction, Collision, Counter-Intuitive Mechanism, Risk-Aware Warning, Curiosity (as section opener) | Framework (works but Newsletter usually builds beyond a single framework), Eloquence (as closer rather than opener) |

## Iteration

This file is a living document. Weekly review:
- Which hook families drove engagement per platform? Note wins.
- Which hooks fell flat? Note and retire — or diagnose (was it the hook, or the post?).
- New formula candidate? Test in 2–3 posts before adding to this file.
- If a hook starts to feel formulaic, rotate it out for a cycle.

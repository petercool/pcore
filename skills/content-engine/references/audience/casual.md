# casual.md — Secondary Audience

## Who They Are

Board-level professionals. Allocators. Consultants. Adjacent operators.

Specifically:
- Institutional allocators (endowments, pensions, family offices) who aren't deep in tech
- Board members and advisors with broad strategic oversight
- Consultants (McKinsey/BCG/Bain types) working on AI/fintech mandates
- Non-technical founders of non-tech businesses
- Senior professionals in adjacent industries (legal, regulatory, policy) who need enough context to ask good questions

They skew 35–60. They have deep expertise — but it's in *their* domain, not necessarily Peter's.

## What They Want

- **Accessibility without dumbing down.** They're smart; they just don't live in this niche daily.
- **Rigor they can trust.** They'll cite Peter in their own meetings. Sloppy claims get them in trouble.
- **Translation of technical to business.** What does this mean for capital allocation, risk, or strategic positioning?
- **Probability frameworks.** They think in scenarios. "Base case 60%, downside 25%, tail 15%" lands well.
- **Specific cases** — they learn through well-chosen examples more than through abstract frameworks.

## What They Don't Want

- Jargon without context. They know what PE and ROE mean. They don't necessarily know what a "data pipeline" is.
- Condescension of any kind. Their expertise is in a different domain — that doesn't make them less capable.
- Pure technical walk-throughs. They want the strategic read, not the implementation.
- Motivational content. Same as builders — they want substance.

## How to Talk to Them

**Same peer respect as with builders — lighter on jargon.** The critical move is translation *without* condescension.

- **Frame technical concepts by analogy to their world.** Not "imagine a simple coin flip," but:
  - Options convexity → insurance pricing
  - Data pipeline latency → supply chain timing
  - Model drift → portfolio rebalancing lag
  These analogies respect their existing expertise rather than introducing "simple" concepts.
- **Expand abbreviations on first use.** "MoM" → "month-over-month (MoM)". After the first use, the short form is fine.
- **Lead with the implication, then the mechanism.** This audience wants the strategic read first, the technical detail second.
- **Use specific cases.** "When [fund] tried this in 2023, here's what happened" works better than abstract principles.
- **Question-ending still works.** But the question should be framed in their domain — "How does this change how your allocators think about AI exposure?" lands better than a pure-technical framing.

## Signals They're Engaged

- DMs asking to discuss offline or book a call — very high signal for this audience
- Forwards to colleagues — high signal
- Comments that connect Peter's point to their own domain ("this maps to [regulatory shift] we're watching") — high signal
- Likes without comments — lower signal than for builders, but still positive (this audience comments less publicly)

## What to Watch For

- If Peter's LinkedIn engagement starts skewing entirely builder-heavy, that's fine — but it means the casual audience isn't growing. Periodically post content explicitly framed for strategic/allocator readers to rebalance.
- If technical posts start getting "ELI5 please" comments, the gap is too wide. Add one sentence of analog framing next time.

## Brand Pillars This Audience Cares Most About

From `brand-voice.md`'s 6 pillars, this audience strongly engages with:

1. **Investor thinking** — very high (their native language)
2. **Market intelligence** — very high
3. **Founder reality** — moderate (they observe it from outside)
4. **AI × finance** — high (but they want the strategic read, not the implementation)
5. **Tools & frameworks** — high (if framed strategically rather than technically)
6. **Technical edge** — lower (unless translated to business implication)

When writing for this audience, lean on pillars 1, 2, 4, 5. Pillar 3 works if framed as "what founders see that allocators miss." Pillar 6 requires translation.

## Dual-Audience Posts

Some posts naturally hit both audiences. When drafting, flag which audience is primary:

- **Builder-primary, casual-accessible** — technical depth, but with one paragraph that translates the strategic implication for allocators. Default mode for most posts.
- **Casual-primary, builder-accessible** — strategic framing, with one section that goes technical for builders who want the mechanism.

Avoid posts that are **neither** — these end up vague and generic.

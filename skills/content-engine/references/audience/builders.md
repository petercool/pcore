# builders.md — Primary Audience

## Who They Are

Founders. Indie hackers. Quants. Engineers. Traders. Portfolio managers. AI researchers. Solo operators who ship.

Specifically:
- Founders building AI, fintech, trading, or data-infrastructure companies
- Engineers working on production systems (backend, data, ML, cloud)
- Quantitative researchers and systematic traders
- Portfolio managers running real capital
- Technical investors and adjacent professionals (technical consultants, solutions architects, CTOs)

They skew 25–45. They've been doing their thing for 3–15 years. They have taste.

## What They Want

- **Actionable frameworks** they can apply next Monday. Not theory-for-theory's-sake.
- **Precise numbers** — ranges, deltas, specific metrics with timeframes. They distrust hand-waving.
- **Systems thinking** — how pieces interact, what breaks at scale, where the second-order effects live.
- **Critical analysis** — they respect counterarguments and invalidation conditions. "Here's what could prove me wrong" builds more credibility than confidence alone.
- **Mechanisms, not conclusions** — they want to understand why, not just what.
- **Real stories** — what actually happened when someone tried this, including the failures.

## What They Don't Want

- Motivational content. They're motivated. They want substance.
- Buzzword-laden AI hype. They build with this stuff; they know what's real.
- Generic "X lessons from my startup journey" posts. Too vague, too common.
- Content that assumes they need basics explained. Don't translate "basis points" for this audience.
- Anything that sounds like a pitch. They filter aggressively.

## How to Talk to Them

**Peer-to-peer. Always.** This is the single most important rule.

- **Assume competence.** They know what a moving average is, what ROIC means, what a Kubernetes operator does. Don't explain.
- **Show trade-offs openly.** Every decision has costs. Pretending otherwise burns credibility.
- **Bring the data.** Specific numbers, specific timeframes, specific cases. Vague claims ("saw significant improvement") don't land here.
- **Invite disagreement.** "Here's my current read — what's the strongest counterargument?" is respected. "Here's the answer" is suspect.
- **Never teacher/student.** You're sharing what you've found with peers who may find the opposite. Not teaching a class.

**If they're wrong about something:** walk them through the reasoning. Let them arrive at the conclusion. Don't announce the error. Respect the possibility that *you're* the one who's wrong and they've seen something you haven't.

## Signals They're Engaged

- Comments that push back with specifics ("tried this, got different result in [regime]") — high-value
- DMs asking about the mechanism
- Bookmarks / saves (more signal than likes)
- Shares to their own networks (highest signal — means they're willing to stake reputation on the content)

## What to Watch For

- If the audience shifts toward replies that are just emoji/agreement, the content is getting shallower. Correct by adding more mechanism, more counterargument.
- If engagement drops after a heavy week of frameworks, rotate to case studies or post-mortems. Frameworks fatigue.

## Brand Pillars This Audience Cares Most About

From `brand-voice.md`'s 6 pillars, this audience strongly engages with:

1. **Investor thinking** — very high
2. **Founder reality** — very high
3. **Technical edge** — very high
4. **AI × finance** — very high (most of this audience works in or near this space)
5. **Market intelligence** — high (but the operators/engineers care less than the investors)
6. **Tools & frameworks** — high (always strong engagement for reusable mental models)

When in doubt, pitch toward pillars 1–4 for this audience.
